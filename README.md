# Quantifying the structure and evolution of the global ecosystem of international organizations

## Abstract
International organizations serve as the cornerstone of global governance, yet they remain severely understudied from a quantitative perspective. Here we leverage a century-spanning, field-comprehensive, and multidimensional dataset of 25,268 international organizations worldwide to chart their structure and evolution. First, we uncover three phases of growth--with explosive growth occurring in the mid-20th century--and capture the existence of a heavy-tailed scale distribution dominated by small organizations, alongside a long-run drift toward smaller new organizations. Second, we map the functional landscape of international organizations, revealing that most operate in one or two fields, hub fields overlap extensively, and field pairs co-occur more often than expected by chance, yielding two functional clusters bridged by technology and engineering. Third, we show how participation is highly stratified, with European and American countries consistently dominating both membership and headquarters, and countries with similar participation profiles preferentially joining the same organizations. Finally, we examine mortality dynamics and find that the average lifespan of dead organizations is only 22.45 years, fundamental fields exhibit greater resilience, mortality spikes around major economic crises, and mortality rates also correlate positively with participation numbers for high- and low-participation country groups. Taken together, these regularities provide a quantitative baseline for analyzing and strengthening global governance.
